How to Become a Lawyer in South Africa: Attorney vs Advocate

Law splits into two paths — attorney and advocate — with very different routes and lifestyles. Here's how to qualify for each.

MzansiInsider7 min readUpdated June 2026

Start with the LLB

The foundation is a four-year LLB degree (or a BA/BCom Law followed by the LLB). It's the common starting point for both attorneys and advocates.

Attorney or Advocate?

Attorneys deal directly with clients and do a two-year articles (training contract); advocates are courtroom specialists who complete a year of pupillage at the Bar, briefed by attorneys. The two carry different risks and rewards.

Admission

Both routes require passing the Legal Practice Council board exams and being admitted by the High Court. Only then can you practise.

"Big Law partners and senior advocates are among the best-paid professionals in the country — but the early years, especially advocates' pupillage, can pay very little."

What Lawyers Earn

Pay is highly polarised. See our lawyer salary guide and our becoming an attorney guide for detail.

Get It Funded

The Department of Justice Law Bursary funds LLB students in exchange for service in the justice system.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to become a lawyer in South Africa?
An LLB takes four years, followed by two years of articles (for attorneys) or a year of pupillage (for advocates), plus passing the board exams and being admitted.
What's the difference between an attorney and an advocate?
Attorneys deal directly with clients and complete articles; advocates are courtroom specialists who complete pupillage at the Bar and are briefed by attorneys.
How much do lawyers earn in South Africa?
Pay is highly polarised — candidate attorneys earn a modest stipend, while Big Law partners and senior advocates are among the best-paid professionals. See our lawyer salary guide.
Can you get a bursary to study law?
Yes. The Department of Justice Law Bursary funds LLB students in exchange for a work-back commitment in the justice system.
