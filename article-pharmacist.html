How to Become a Pharmacist in South Africa

Pharmacy is a scarce skill with steady demand across retail, hospital, and industry. Here's the path from BPharm to registered pharmacist.

MzansiInsider6 min readUpdated June 2026

The BPharm Degree

Becoming a pharmacist starts with a four-year Bachelor of Pharmacy (BPharm) degree at an accredited university. Strong Maths and Physical Science marks are needed for entry.

Internship and Community Service

After your degree you complete a year of internship and a year of community service before you can register and practise independently.

Register with the SAPC

Registration with the South African Pharmacy Council (SAPC) is mandatory to practise. It's the body that licenses pharmacists and upholds professional standards.

"Pharmacy ownership and the pharmaceutical industry — not just retail and hospital roles — are where the highest pharmacist earnings sit."

Where Pharmacists Work

Retail chains like Clicks and Dis-Chem, hospitals, and the pharmaceutical industry. Ownership and industry roles pay the most. See our pharmacist salary guide.

Get Started

Apply for a BPharm and secure funding through NSFAS or a healthcare bursary.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to become a pharmacist?
Six years in total: a four-year BPharm degree, a one-year internship, and a one-year community service placement before full registration.
Do pharmacists register with the SAPC?
Yes. Registration with the South African Pharmacy Council (SAPC) is mandatory to practise as a pharmacist in South Africa.
Where do pharmacists earn the most?
The pharmaceutical industry and pharmacy ownership offer the highest earnings, followed by hospital and retail management roles.
Is pharmacy a scarce skill?
Yes. Pharmacy is recognised as a scarce-skill profession with steady demand across retail, hospital, and industry settings.
