Delivery & E-hailing in South Africa

One of the lowest-barrier side hustles in South Africa — if you have a bicycle, scooter or car and a smartphone, you can start earning within days.

Overview

Delivery platforms like Bolt Food, Uber Eats, and Mr D actively recruit delivery partners across most cities and many towns. E-hailing driving with Uber or Bolt pays more but requires a roadworthy car and the relevant permits.

The appeal is flexibility and a fast start: you choose your hours, and onboarding is quick compared with most other income sources.

Realistic Income Breakdown

Part-time food delivery realistically brings R3 000–R8 000 a month depending on hours, area, and tips. Full e-hailing driving with a car earns more — roughly R5 000–R15 000 a month after costs — though fuel, data, and vehicle wear eat into the gross, so tracking real profit matters.

How to Start

Step by step
1
Choose your platform — Bolt Food, Uber Eats, or Mr D for delivery; Uber/Bolt for driving
2
Gather your documents: ID, a valid licence (for driving), and banking details
3
Register on the platform and complete onboarding and vehicle checks
4
Start during busy periods (evenings, weekends) to maximise earnings
5
Track fuel, data, and maintenance so you know your real profit

Frequently Asked Questions

How much can you earn delivering in South Africa?
Part-time food delivery realistically brings R3 000–R8 000 a month, while full e-hailing driving with a car earns roughly R5 000–R15 000 a month after costs.
What do you need to start delivery work?
A bicycle, scooter, or car, a smartphone, and the platform's required documents. E-hailing driving also needs a valid licence and a roadworthy, registered vehicle.
Do delivery costs eat into earnings?
Yes. Fuel, data, and vehicle wear reduce your gross, so it's important to track real profit rather than just turnover.
Can you do delivery without a car?
Yes. Many platforms allow bicycle or scooter delivery, and you can also lease your vehicle to a vetted driver for more passive income.

Don't Forget SARS

Side-hustle income is taxable. Keep records from day one, separate business from personal money, and set aside a portion for tax. Once you earn beyond the threshold you may need to register as a provisional taxpayer.
