How to Become a Doctor in South Africa: MBChB, Specialisation, and Public vs Private Practice

From matric requirements to community service, registrar posts, and the financial reality of a South African medical career — everything prospective doctors need to know.

MzansiInsider11 min readUpdated June 2026

Matric Requirements

You'll need strong results in Mathematics, Physical Sciences, and Life Sciences — most medical schools expect a minimum of 70% across the key subjects, and competition pushes the real cut-off higher. The National Benchmark Test (NBT) is also required.

The MBChB Degree

A six-year Bachelor of Medicine and Surgery at one of South Africa's medical schools (UCT, Wits, Stellenbosch, UP, UKZN, UFS, Sefako Makgatho, Walter Sisulu). Places are limited and heavily oversubscribed.

Internship and Community Service

After graduating, two years of internship followed by one year of compulsory community service ("comm serve") in the public system, often in rural or under-resourced areas. Only after this are you fully registered with the HPCSA as an independent practitioner.

"The training is long and the public-sector hours are punishing, but few careers offer the same combination of security, meaning, and earning potential."

Specialisation

To specialise, you compete for a registrar post and complete four to six further years of training plus college exams. Specialties like surgery, cardiology, and radiology are the most competitive — and the most lucrative.

The Financial Reality

  • Medical Officer (GP, public): R480,000 – R1,000,000/year
  • GP (private practice): R700,000 – R1,500,000/year
  • Specialist Surgeon: R1.4m – R3.5m+/year

Private practice pays substantially more but carries business risk and overheads. Many doctors blend public-sector security with private sessional work.
