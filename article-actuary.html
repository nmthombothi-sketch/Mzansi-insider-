How to Become an Actuary in South Africa: Exams, Fellowship and Pay

Actuarial science is among the highest-paid professions in the country — but the road to qualifying is long and exam-heavy. Here's exactly what it takes.

MzansiInsider8 min readUpdated June 2026

What Actuaries Do

Actuaries use mathematics, statistics, and financial theory to measure and manage risk — mostly in insurance, pensions, banking, and investments. It's a profession built on rigour, and one of the few where the qualification itself dramatically lifts your earning power.

Start with the Right Degree

The standard route is a BSc in Actuarial Science (or maths/statistics) at a recognised university. Exceptional matric results — especially in Mathematics — are essential, since the degree and the exams that follow are mathematically intense.

The ASSA Exams

Qualifying means passing a long series of professional exams set by the Actuarial Society of South Africa (ASSA). Students typically write these while working, and each set of exams passed lifts earnings. Becoming a Fellow (FASSA) usually takes six to ten years in total.

"Every set of actuarial exams you pass moves your salary up — but qualifying as a Fellow is a marathon that demands years of disciplined study alongside full-time work."

Get It Funded

Actuarial bursaries are generous because the skills are scarce. The Discovery Actuarial Bursary and others fund top students and support them through the exams.

Is It Worth It?

Financially, yes. Actuaries are among the highest earners in South Africa, with strong demand from insurers, pension funds, and banks. The trade-off is the long, difficult examination process — see our actuary salary guide for the numbers.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to become an actuary in South Africa?
Usually six to ten years: a three-year BSc in actuarial science or maths, followed by a demanding series of professional exams through the Actuarial Society of South Africa, completed while working.
What exams do actuaries write?
Actuaries write a long series of professional exams set by the Actuarial Society of South Africa (ASSA), progressing toward Fellowship (FASSA), the full professional qualification.
Is actuarial science worth it?
Financially, yes — actuaries are among the highest-paid professionals in South Africa, with strong demand. The trade-off is a long and difficult examination process.
What do you need to study actuarial science?
Exceptional matric results, especially in Mathematics, and acceptance into a BSc Actuarial Science (or maths/statistics) programme at a recognised university.
